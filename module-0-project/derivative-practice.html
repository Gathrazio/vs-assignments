Q. 1:

$$\frac{d}{dx}(x-4)^3\;\; = \;\;\;?$$
We can compute this derivative in two ways. The first involves identifying a function composition -- i.e., if \(f(x) = x^3\) and \(g(x) = x-4\), then we are computing \(\frac{d}{dx} f\circ g(x) = \frac{d}{dx} f(g(x)) = f\prime(g(x))g\prime(x)\) via the chain rule (and power rule for \(f\prime\)): $$\frac{d}{dx}(x-4)^3$$ $$=$$ $$3(x-4)^{3\;-1}(1)$$ $$=$$ $$\boxed{3\;(x-4)^2}.$$ See the Resources button for more information. The alternative way, which does not require the chain rule, is to simply distribute the polynomial and use the power rule along with the linearity of the derivative: $$\begin{align*}&\;\;\;\;\frac{d}{dx}(x-4)^3\\ &= \frac{d}{dx}\left((x-4)^2(x-4)\right)\\ &=\frac{d}{dx}\left((x^2-8x+16)(x-4)\right)\\ &=\frac{d}{dx}\left((x^3 - 8x^2 + 16x) + (-4x^2 +32x - 64)\right)\\ &=\frac{d}{dx}\left(x^3 - 12x^2 + 48x - 64\right)\\ &=\frac{d}{dx}x^3 - 12\frac{d}{dx}x^2 + 48\frac{d}{dx}x - \frac{d}{dx}64\\ &=3x^2 -24x+48\\ &=3\;(x^2 - 8x + 16)\\ &\stackrel{\checkmark}{=}\boxed{3\;(x-4)^2}.\end{align*}$$

Q. 2:

$$\frac{d}{dx}\ln(\sin(\cos\;(2x))) \;\;= \;\;\;?$$
This derivative really requires us to sort out our understanding of the chain rule. We shall compute this derivative in sections. First, the derivative of \(\ln(x)\) is \(\frac{d}{dx}\ln(x) = \frac{1}{x}\). So the derivative of \(\ln(f(x))\) is \(\frac{d}{dx}\ln(f(x)) = \frac{1}{f(x)}f\prime(x)\). Hence $$\frac{d}{dx}\ln(\sin(\cos\;(2x)))$$ $$=$$ $$\frac{1}{\sin(\cos\;(2x))}\frac{d}{dx}\sin(\cos\;(2x)).$$ We compute this derivative next. We know that \(\frac{d}{dx}\sin(x) = \cos\;(x)\), so \(\frac{d}{dx}\sin(g(x)) = \cos\;(g(x))g\prime(x)\). Thus $$\frac{d}{dx}\sin(\cos\;(2x))$$ $$=$$ $$\cos\;(\cos\;(2x))\frac{d}{dx}\cos\;(2x).$$ And finally, as \(\frac{d}{dx}\cos\;(x)=-\sin(x)\), it follows that \(\frac{d}{dx}\cos\;(q(x)) = -\sin(q(x))q\prime(x)\). Hence $$\frac{d}{dx}\cos\;(2x)$$ $$=$$ $$-\sin(2x)(2) = -2\sin(2x).$$ Putting this all together, the solution follows: $$\begin{align*} &\;\;\;\;\frac{d}{dx}\ln(\sin(\cos\;(2x)))\\ &=\frac{1}{\sin(\cos\;(2x))}\frac{d}{dx}\sin(\cos\;(2x))\\ &=\frac{\cos\;(\cos\;(2x))}{\sin(\cos\;(2x))}\frac{d}{dx}\cos\;(2x)\\ &=\boxed{\frac{-2\sin(2x)\cos\;(\cos\;(2x))}{\sin(\cos\;(2x))}}.\end{align*}$$

Q. 3:

$$\frac{d}{dx}5x^2e^x\;\; = \;\;\;?$$
Here we use the product rule. Namely, $$\begin{align*}&\;\;\;\;\frac{d}{dx}5x^2e^x\\ &= 5x^2\frac{d}{dx}e^x + e^x\frac{d}{dx}5x^2 \\ &= 5x^2e^x + 10xe^x\\ &=\boxed{5xe^x(x+2)}. \end{align*}$$

Q. 4:

$$\frac{d}{dx}\left(\frac{x^6+2}{\sin(x)}\right)\;\; = \;\;\;?$$
Here we must use the quotient rule: \begin{align*} &\;\;\;\;\frac{d}{dx}\left(\frac{x^6+2}{\sin(x)}\right)\\ &= \frac{\sin(x)\frac{d}{dx}(x^6+2)- (x^6+2)\frac{d}{dx}\sin(x)}{\sin^2(x)}\\\ &= \boxed{\frac{6x^5\sin(x) - (x^6+2)\cos(x)}{\sin^2(x)}}. \end{align*}

Q. 5:

$$\frac{d}{dx}x^\pi\pi^x2^{4x}\;\;= \;\;\;?$$
As can be seen, we are trying to compute the derivative of the product of three functions of \(x\). This will require us to nest applications of the product rule, and to recall the derivative of an exponent: $$\frac{d}{dx}b^x = b^x \ln(b).$$ Thus, we proceed as follows (noting that the \(2^{4x}\) term will require the chain rule as well) \begin{align*} &\;\;\;\;\frac{d}{dx}x^\pi\pi^x2^{4x}\\ &= x^{\pi}\frac{d}{dx}(\pi^x 2^{4x}) + \pi^x2^{4x}\frac{d}{dx}x^\pi\\ &=x^\pi \left(\pi^x\frac{d}{dx}2^{4x}+2^{4x}\frac{d}{dx}\pi^x\right) + \pi^x2^{4x}\left(\pi x^{\pi-1}\right)\\ &=x^\pi\left(\pi^x2^{4x}\ln(2)(4) + 2^{4x}\pi^x \ln(\pi)\right)+\pi^{x+1}2^{4x}x^{\pi-1}\\ &=\boxed{x^{\pi-1}\pi^x2^{4x}\left(4x\ln(2) + x\ln(\pi)+\pi\right)}. \end{align*}

Q. 6:

$$\frac{d}{dx}\ln(x)^{x\ln(x)}\;\; = \;\;\;?$$
The viable technique here is logarithmic differentiation, as it will be easier to take the derivative of the log of this function rather than the function itself. We can name this function \(y = \ln(x)^{x\ln(x)}\) and take the natural log of both sides: $$\ln(y) = x\ln(x)\ln(\ln(x)).$$ We now take the derivative of both sides with respect to \(x\): $$\begin{align*}&\;\;\;\;\frac{d}{dx}\ln(y)\\ &= \frac{1}{y}\frac{dy}{dx}\\ &=\frac{d}{dx}x\ln(x)\ln(\ln(x))\\ &= (x\ln(x))\left(\frac{1}{\ln(x)}\frac{1}{x}\right)+ \ln(\ln(x))\left(x\left(\frac{1}{x}\right) + \ln(x)\right)\\ &=1 + (\ln(x) + 1)\ln(\ln(x)). \end{align*}$$ We now just solve for \(y\prime = \frac{dy}{dx}\), which is the desired derivative. Note that \(y\) is still a function of \(x\), so we can replace it at the end: $$\begin{align*}\frac{dy}{dx} &= y\;[1 + (\ln(x) + 1)\ln(\ln(x))]\\ & = \boxed{\ln(x)^{x\ln(x)}[1+(\ln(x)+1)\ln(\ln(x))]}. \end{align*}$$

Q. 7:

$$\frac{d}{dx}\frac{1}{\log_{10}(\sec(x))}\;\; = \;\;\;?$$
Here we use the quotient rule, the chain rule, the derivative of a log with an arbitrary base, and the derivative of secant being secant tangent: $$\begin{align*} &\;\;\;\;\frac{d}{dx}\frac{1}{\log_{10}(\sec(x))}\\ &= \frac{\log_{10}(\sec(x))(0) - \frac{d}{dx}\log_{10}(\sec(x))}{\log_{10}(\sec(x))^2}\\ &=\frac{-\frac{1}{\sec(x)\ln(10)}\frac{d}{dx}\sec(x)}{\log_{10}(\sec(x))^2}\\ &=\boxed{\frac{-\tan(x)}{\ln(10)\log_{10}(\sec(x))^2}}. \end{align*}$$

Q. 8:

$$\frac{d}{dx}\left(7-\arcsin\left(\frac{1}{x}\right)\right)^5 \;\;= \;\;\;?$$
The derivative of arcsine is as follows: $$\frac{d}{dx}\arcsin(x) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1-x^2}}.$$ We can then use the power rule, the chain rule, and this computation for the derivative: $$\begin{align*}&\;\;\;\;\frac{d}{dx}\left(7-\arcsin\left(\frac{1}{x}\right)\right)^5\\ &= 5\left(7-\arcsin\left(\frac{1}{x}\right)\right)^4\left(-\frac{d}{dx}\arcsin\left(\frac{1}{x}\right)\right)\\ &=5\left(7-\arcsin\left(\frac{1}{x}\right)\right)^4\frac{1}{\sqrt{1-\left(\frac{1}{x}\right)^2}}\left(\frac{1}{x^2}\right)\\ &=\boxed{5\left(7-\arcsin\left(\frac{1}{x}\right)\right)^4\frac{1}{x^2\sqrt{1-\left(\frac{1}{x}\right)^2}}}. \end{align*}$$

Q. 9:

$$\frac{d}{dx}\left(\frac{1}{x}+\frac{2}{x^2}+ \frac{3}{x^3}\right)\;\; = \;\;\;?$$
We have here some simple power rule applications. Namely, $$\begin{align*} &\;\;\;\;\frac{d}{dx}\left(\frac{1}{x}+\frac{2}{x^2}+ \frac{3}{x^3}\right)\\ &= \frac{d}{dx}(x^{-1}+2x^{-2}+3x^{-3})\\ &=\frac{d}{dx}x^{-1} + 2\frac{d}{dx}x^{-2}+3\frac{d}{dx}x^{-3}\\ &=-x^{-2} -4x^{-3}-9x^{-4}\\ &=\boxed{-\frac{1}{x^2}-\frac{4}{x^3}-\frac{9}{x^4}}. \end{align*}$$

Q. 10:

$$\frac{d}{dx}((x+2)^3(x+3)^2)^6\;\; = \;\;\;?$$
Here we use several application of the power rule, chain rule, and product rule: $$\begin{align*} &\;\;\;\;\frac{d}{dx}((x+2)^3(x+3)^2)^6\\ &= 6((x+2)^3(x+3)^2)^5\frac{d}{dx}[(x+2)^3(x+3)^2]\\ &=6(x+2)^{15}(x+3)^{11}(x+2)^2(x+3)[2(x+2)+3(x+3)]\\ &=\boxed{6\;(x+2)^{17}(x+3)^{11}(5x+13)}. \end{align*}$$ We could also potentially distribute the outer exponent and use the product rule: $$\begin{align*} &\;\;\;\;\frac{d}{dx}((x+2)^3(x+3)^2)^6\\ &= \frac{d}{dx}(x+2)^{18}(x+3)^{12}\\ &=(x+2)^{18}(12)(x+3)^{11} + (x+3)^{12}(18)(x+2)^{17}\\ &=\boxed{6\;(x+2)^{17}(x+3)^{11}(5x+13)}. \end{align*}$$
