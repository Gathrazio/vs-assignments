Integral Practice!

Q. 1:

$$\int xe^{x^2}dx\;\; = \;\;\;?$$
We can use \(u\)-substitution here, where \(u=x^2\). Thus \(\frac{1}{2}du=xdx\), which means $$\begin{align*}\int xe^{x^2}dx &= \frac{1}{2}\int e^u du\\&=\frac{1}{2}e^u + C\\ &=\boxed{\frac{1}{2}e^{x^2} + C}.\end{align*}$$

Q. 2:

$$\int_0^1 x^2 dx \;\;= \;\;\;?$$
In this case the reverse power rule works nicely. Also, do not forget to evaluate the antiderivative at the bounds of the integral like so: $$\begin{align*}\int_0^1x^2dx &= \frac{1}{3}x^3\bigg\rvert_0^1\\ &=\frac{1}{3}\left[1^3-0^3\right]\\ &=\boxed{\frac{1}{3}}. \end{align*}$$

Q. 3:

$$\int\left[\frac{4}{(x+2)\ln(x+2)}\right]dx\;\; = \;\;\;?$$
For this problem, it is good to notice that we have the derivative of \(\ln(x+2)\) in the integrand product. This should encourage us to perform a \(u\)-substitution with \(\ln(x+2)\), namely \(u = \ln(x+2)\), which implies that \(du = \frac{dx}{x+2}\). The integral then becomes $$\begin{align*}&\;\;\;\;\int\left[\frac{4}{(x+2)\ln(x+2)}\right]dx\\ &= 4\int\left[\frac{1}{u}\right]du\\ &=4\ln|\;u| + C\\&=\boxed{4\ln|\ln(x+2)| \;+ C}.\end{align*}$$

Q. 4:

$$\int e^x \tan^2\left(e^x\right)\sec^2\left(e^x\right)dx\;\; = \;\;\;?$$
It is important to recall that \(\frac{d}{dx}\tan(x) = \sec^2(x)\). Knowing this, and being sure not to forget the chain rule, we can see that \(\frac{d}{dx}\tan(e^x) = e^x\sec^2(e^x)\), which appears in the integrand! So we shall perform a \(u\)-sub with \(u = \tan(e^x)\) and \(du = e^x\sec^2(x)dx\): $$\begin{align*}&\;\;\;\;\int e^x \tan^2(e^x) \sec^2(e^x) dx\\ &= \int \tan^2(e^x)e^x\sec^2(x)dx\\ &=\int u^2 du\\ &=\frac{1}{3}u^3 + C\\ &=\boxed{\frac{1}{3}\tan^3(e^x)+ C}.\end{align*}$$

Q. 5:

$$\int_0^4xe^{\frac{x}{2}}dx \;\;= \;\;\;?$$
This integral is a bit different in that we need to use integration by parts. Recall the definite integral version of the integration by parts formula below, where \(u\) and \(v\) are functions of \(x\): $$\int_a^b udv = uv\bigg\rvert_a^b - \int_a^b v du$$ If we make the substitutions \(u = x\) and \( v = 2e^{\frac{x}{2}}\), we have this form! Namely, it follows that \(du = dx\) and \(dv = e^{\frac{x}{2}}dx\), so the whole integral can be computed like so: $$\begin{align*}&\;\;\;\;\int_0^4 xe^{\frac{x}{2}}dx\\ &= \int_0^4 udv\\ &=uv\bigg\rvert_0^4 - \int_0^4 vdu\\ &=2xe^{\frac{x}{2}}\bigg\rvert_0^4 - \int_0^4 2e^{\frac{x}{2}}\\ &=8e^2 - 4e^{\frac{x}{2}}dx\\ &=8e^2 - 4\;(e^2-1)\\ &=\boxed{4\;(e^2+1)}.\end{align*}$$ For those of you who know multivariable calculus, we are not able to integrate like so $$\int udv = uv\;+C$$ precisely because \(u\) and \(v\) are not independent variables.

Q. 6:

$$\int \sec(\theta)d\theta\;\; = \;\;\;?$$
Deceptively simple, the key to this integral is multiplication by a special form of 1 followed by a clever \(u\)-substitution. We proceed as follows: $$\begin{align*}&\;\;\;\;\int\sec(\theta)dx\\ &= \int \frac{\sec(\theta)[\sec(\theta)+\tan(\theta)]}{\sec(\theta)+\tan(\theta)}d\theta\\ &=\int \frac{\sec^2(\theta) + \sec(\theta)\tan(\theta)}{\sec(\theta) + \tan(\theta)}d\theta.\end{align*}$$ At this point, we can make the \(u\)-substitution \(u=\sec(\theta) + \tan(\theta)\), which implies that \(du = (\sec(\theta)\tan(\theta) + \sec^2(\theta))d\theta\). This makes the ending clear: $$\begin{align*} &\;\;\;\;\int\sec(\theta)d\theta\\ &= \int \frac{\sec^2(\theta) + \sec(\theta)\tan(\theta)}{\sec(\theta) + \tan(\theta)}d\theta\\ &=\int \frac{du}{u}\\ &=\ln|\;u| +C\\ &=\boxed{\ln |\;\sec(\theta)+\tan(\theta)|\;+C}. \end{align*}$$

Q. 7:

$$\int_0^\infty \frac{x^2}{x^6+1}dx\;\; = \;\;\;?$$
This is an improper integral, which means we must rewrite with limit notation. We can also identify the \(u\)-substitution \(u = x^3\) and \(\frac{1}{3}du = x^2dx\). It would also be useful to re-familiarize ourselves with the conventionally accepted definition (including domain and range) of the arctangent function: $$\begin{align*}&\;\;\;\;\int_0^\infty \frac{x^2}{x^6+1}dx\\ &= \lim_{b\to\infty}\int_0^b \frac{x^2}{x^6+1}dx\\ &=\frac{1}{3}\lim_{b\to\infty} \int_0^b\frac{1}{u^2+1}du\\ &=\frac{1}{3}\lim_{b\to\infty} \Bigg[\arctan(u)\bigg\rvert_0^b\Bigg]\\ &=\frac{1}{3}\lim_{b\to\infty}\left[\arctan(b) - \arctan(0)\right]\\ &=\frac{1}{3}\left[\frac{\pi}{2}-0\right]\\ &=\boxed{\frac{\pi}{6}}.\end{align*}$$

Q. 8:

$$\int \csc(\theta)d\theta \;\;= \;\;\;?$$
The solution to this one is a mirror of the \(\sec(\theta)\) integral in Question 6; we instead here use a slightly different form of 1 and the \(u\)-substitution \(u = \csc(\theta) + \cot(\theta)\), \(\;\;du = -(\csc(\theta)\cot(\theta)+\csc^2(\theta))d\theta\): $$\begin{align*}&\;\;\;\;\int\csc(\theta)d\theta\\ &= \int \frac{\csc(\theta)(\csc(\theta)+\cot(\theta))}{\csc(\theta)+\cot(\theta)}d\theta\\ &=\int\frac{\csc^2(\theta) + \csc(\theta)\cot(\theta)}{\csc(\theta)+\cot(\theta)}d\theta\\ &=-\int \frac{du}{u}\\ &=-\ln|u| + C\\ &= \boxed{-\ln|\;\csc(\theta) + \cot(\theta)|\; + C}.\end{align*}$$

Q. 9:

$$\int \frac{1}{x\sqrt{16-9x^2}}dx\;\; = \;\;\;?$$
This integral is one that will require trigonometric substitution, as the square root looks like something related to the Pythagorean Identity. That is, we can manipulate the square root into a form attainable by the Pythagorean Identity: $$\sqrt{16-9x^2} = 4\sqrt{1-\left(\frac{3x}{4}\right)^2}.$$ Manipulating the Pythagorean Identity, we see we have an identical form that will reveal to us the correct trig substitution: $$\sin^2(\theta) + \cos^2(\theta) = 1$$ $$\iff$$ $$\cos(\theta) = \sqrt{1-[\sin(\theta)]^2}.$$ Hence the trig sub we choose is \(\sin(\theta) = \frac{3x}{4} \iff x = \frac{4}{3}\sin(\theta)\) and \(dx = \frac{4}{3}\cos(\theta)d\theta\). We will also use the solution to Question 8: $$\begin{align*}&\;\;\;\;\int \frac{1}{x\sqrt{16 - 9x^2}}dx\\ &= \int \frac{1}{4x\sqrt{1-\left(\frac{3x}{4}\right)^2}}dx\\ &=\int\frac{\frac{4}{3}\cos(\theta)d\theta}{4\left(\frac{4}{3}\sin(\theta)\right)\sqrt{1-\sin^2(\theta)}}\\ &=\frac{1}{4}\int \frac{1}{\sin(\theta)}d\theta\\ &=\frac{1}{4}\int \csc(\theta)d\theta\\ &=-\frac{1}{4}\ln|\csc(\theta)+\cot(\theta)| \;+ C.\end{align*}$$ We aren't done yet though. If we draw a right triangle to represent our original substitution, we would draw our angle \(\theta\) as one of the acute angles, \(3|\;x|\) as the opposite side of said angle, and \(4\) as the hypotenuse of the triangle. The Pythagorean Theorem says that the adjacent side must be \(\sqrt{16-9x^2}\). Note that the final side in this process will always be the square root in the original problem statement. What is \(\csc(\theta)\) in terms of \(x\)? Well, from our original substitution, \(\csc(\theta) = \frac{1}{\sin(\theta)} = \frac{4}{3|\;x|}\) as \(\theta \in (0, \frac{\pi}{2})\). What is \(\cot(\theta)\) in terms of \(x\)? Since cotangent is adjacent over opposite, it follows that \(\cot(\theta) = \frac{\sqrt{16-9x^2}}{3|\;x|}\). Our solution can then be simplified using a log rule: $$\begin{align*}&\;\;\;\;\int \frac{1}{x\sqrt{16 - 9x^2}}dx\\ &=-\frac{1}{4}\ln|\;\csc(\theta)+\cot(\theta)| \;+ C\\ &=-\frac{1}{4}\ln\bigg\rvert\frac{4}{3|\;x|} + \frac{\sqrt{16-9x^2}}{3|\;x|}\bigg\rvert\;+C\\ &=-\frac{1}{4}\ln\bigg\rvert\frac{1}{3}\left[\frac{4}{|\;x|} + \frac{\sqrt{16-9x^2}}{|\;x|}\right]\bigg\rvert \;+C\\ &=-\frac{1}{4}\ln\left(\frac{1}{3}\right) - \frac{1}{4}\ln\bigg\rvert\frac{4}{|\;x|} + \frac{\sqrt{16-9x^2}}{|\;x|}\bigg\rvert \;+C\\ &=\boxed{-\frac{1}{4}\ln\bigg\rvert \frac{4}{|\;x|} + \frac{\sqrt{16-9x^2}}{|\;x|}\bigg\rvert \;+D}.\end{align*}$$ \(D\) in this case is just another arbitrary constant to represent the fact that \(C\) absorbed the constant we pulled out.

Q. 10:

$$\int\frac{x+1}{(x+3)(x+2)}dx\;\; = \;\;\;?$$
Even if we distributed the denominator, the integrand would still not be in a form fit for \(u\)-substitution. It looks like we can break this quotient apart by solving a system of equations (see partial fraction decomposition). What we are thinking is that $$\frac{x+1}{(x+3)(x+2)}$$ $$=$$ $$\frac{a}{x+3}+\frac{b}{x+2}$$ where \(a\) and \(b\) are constants to be determined. If we find like-denominators on the right side and equate numerators, we obtain the following system: $$\begin{cases}a+b &=& 1\\2a+3b &=& 1\;.\end{cases}$$ Hence \(a=2\) and \(b=-1\;\). We can then compute the integral as follows: $$\begin{align*} &\;\;\;\;\int\frac{x+1}{(x+3)(x+2)}dx\\ &= \int \left(\frac{2}{x+3} - \frac{1}{x+2}\right)dx\\ &=\boxed{2\ln(x+3) - \ln(x+2) + C}. \end{align*}$$
